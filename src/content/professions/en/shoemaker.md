<p>
  Before shoes became widespread, farmers wore "tsirovoulia"—a style resembling traditional
  <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsarouchi" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Greek tsarouchia</a>. These were handmade by families or crafted by "tsivourlades" (shoemakers).
</p>
<h3>The profession of shoemakers</h3>
<p>
  Until the 1950s–60s, shoemaking thrived as a lucrative trade. With few factories, most families relied on local
  artisans. A pair of soles cost 80 drachmas—more than a worker's daily wage of 70 drachmas—prompting many to apprentice
  their children.
</p>
<p>
  Training often began young, as schooling was rare for 19th- to mid-20th-century children. Chios hosted multiple
  shoemakers, with at least one per village and up to three in larger settlements. Lesvos mirrored this pattern.
</p>
<h3>"Papoutsades" or "Tsagkarades"?</h3>
<p>
  Shoemakers fell into two categories: "papoutsades" (crafters of new shoes) and "tsagkarades" (repair specialists).
  While overlapping in skills, most chose a niche. "Papoutsades" earned more per order but had fewer clients than
  repairers.
</p>
<h2>The shoemakers ("papoutsades") of Lesvos</h2>
<p>
  Shoemakers were vital to rural Lesvos, where families bought new footwear only when old pairs were irreparable.
  Artisans avoided competition by specializing—one crafted "scoliana" (elegant heels/slippers), another "rustic" work
  shoes. As the saying went, each had "their customers and their art."
</p>
<h3>Old unions of the shoemakers in Lesvos and Chios</h3>
<p>
  Chios' shoemakers organized into three guilds: luxury shoemakers (patron: St. Dimitris), rustic shoemakers (patron:
  St. Diomedes), and apprentices. Each guild built its own church, with apprentices joining both saints' celebrations.
  Over time, the luxury and rustic guilds merged, honoring both saints. Mytilene had a similar association.
</p>
<h3>The shoemaker's tools and materials</h3>
<h4>Tools</h4>
<p>
  The "kalapodi"—a wooden shoe mold—was central. Shapes varied: pointed, feminine, masculine. Orders were tracked via a
  numbered "mother" system. Tools included wooden tacks, "katsambroki," "souvli" (skewer), small/large pliers ("cat"),
  hooks, files, and shoemaking knives.
</p>
<h4>Materials</h4>
<p>
  Leather, wax, and glue were primary materials. Crafting a pair took roughly two days.
</p>
<h3>The traditional shoemaking process</h3>
<p>
  Shoemaking began with selecting leather based on the client's budget. Artisans traced the "kalapodi" mold onto paper,
  transferred it to leather, and cut the "stampo" (template). The "fontieri" (specialist) sewed the "fontia" (upper
  sections). A thin leather "skin" formed the sole, attached with wooden pegs. After gluing and drying, excess leather was
  trimmed, wrinkles hammered smooth, and soles stitched for durability. Finally, shoes were brushed and waxed.
</p>
<h3>Today</h3>
<p>
  Industrialization eclipsed traditional shoemaking, but a few elderly craftsmen persist in Lesvos, repairing
  footwear—sometimes with ancestral tools—in small, weathered shops.
</p>
