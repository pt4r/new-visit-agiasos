<p>
  Among the lost professions of Lesvos, tailoring stands out—a craft that evolved through eras and earned many names.
  Once a novel trade, it became a family legacy passed from father to son, even shaping surnames.
</p>
<h3>The profession of the tailor and the French influence</h3>
<p>
  Early tailors crafted skirts, shirts, and accessories for shepherds and villagers. As Greek society Europeanized, so
  did its fashion. French styles surged in popularity, birthing a new class of artisans: "frangoraftades" (Francophile
  tailors). These itinerant craftsmen traveled villages with bags of modern drill cloth, distinct from traditional
  loom-woven fabrics.
</p>
<p>
  Specializations emerged: some tailors focused on nuns' robes, while Vlach artisans mastered the "fustanellas"
  (traditional pleated kilts). Though sewing remained a household duty for women, professional tailors thrived in niche
  markets.
</p>
<p>
  Historian Panagiotis Samaras notes that Mytilene's oldest guilds included tailors, furriers, and silk-makers
  ("Kazazides").
</p>
<h3>Materials used and the specialties of the tailors</h3>
<p>
  Tailors worked with felt (earning the name "tsochatzides") or "amba"—a thick, imported fabric dyed blue and red in
  Eastern Romulia. Garments were stitched with silk thread ("brisimi"), adorned with embroidery and silk buttons.
</p>
<p>
  In the early 19th century, "tsochatzides" were essential for crafting "salvaria" (baggy trousers), vests, and
  kaftans. By mid-century, "ambatzides" using "amba" fabric eclipsed them. Complex garments required master tailors.
</p>
<h3>The tailor's tools</h3>
<p>
  Essential tools included thimbles, scissors, measuring tapes, and buckles—metal hooks to secure fabric during
  stitching.
</p>
