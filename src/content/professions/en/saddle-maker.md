<p>
  Until World War II, people and goods traversed Lesvos' steep terrain and underdeveloped road network almost
  exclusively by animal transport. The island's mountainous landscape made mechanized travel impractical.
</p>
<p>
  Mules and donkeys were the primary means of transportation—a reliance that persists today on steep, inaccessible
  slopes within olive groves.
</p>
<h3>The profession of the saddler</h3>
<p>
  Saddlers crafted simple, functional saddles for mules and rare horses. Everyday saddles featured wooden frames lined
  with leather or "ketse" (wool padding). For festivals and races, owners adorned saddles with vibrant fabrics, beads, and
  intricate designs.
</p>
<p>
  "Kapistrades" (saddle-makers) specialized in crafting "kapistrias" (saddles) and "kaploudetes" (straps securing the
  saddle around the animal's hindquarters). This skill was shared by saddlers and shoemakers alike.
</p>
<p>
  Saddlers thrived in Lesvos' large farming communities—notably Mantamado, Agia Paraskevi, and Agiasos. Saddles
  imported from neighboring Chios were also highly prized.
</p>
<h3>Today</h3>
<p>
  This centuries-old craft has nearly vanished, surviving only in memory and historical accounts.
</p>
