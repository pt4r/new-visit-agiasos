They say that the biggest celebration of the year in Agiasos, Lesvos, is that of the Virgin Mary, on August 15th. Something that is still true, but in recent years the “Chestnut Festival” has also become quite popular. In recent years, the “Chestnut Festival”, the biggest celebration of the autumn nature of Lesvos, has been an occasion for residents of the island and not, for a mountain escape to the narrow streets of Agiasos under the fragrance of roasted chestnuts, but mainly with a tour of the paths of its Chestnut Grove, which on such days is flooded with chestnuts and at the same time with the colorful fallen leaves of the chestnut trees that create images of a fairy tale.

## The chestnut tree in Greece

Paleontological data confirm that the chestnut tree has existed since the Tertiary period in Europe, 65-70 million years ago. At that time, temperatures were favorable, which is why the tree had spread northward. This is confirmed by traces of chestnut leaves and fruits found in fossils from the Scandinavian Peninsula. Around the same time, chestnut fossils were found in present-day Canada, Japan, France, Italy, the former Yugoslavia, Austria and Hungary.

Over time and with changing climatic conditions, the chestnut tree's range has been limited to Europe from Spain and Portugal to the Caucasus and includes all the countries bordering the Mediterranean Sea. Today, the main chestnut-producing countries in Europe are Italy, France, Spain, Portugal and Greece.

## How did the chestnut arrive in Agiasos?

According to Stratis P. Kolaxizelis, during the Roman occupation of Lesbos (88 BC – 395 AD), one of the rulers of the “land of Penthili” brought the first chestnut trees from Asia Minor and planted them in the area. After the terrible frost that hit the olive groves and went down in history as “the blight of olives” (11-1-1850), the locals realized that they should not limit themselves to olive cultivation alone, but should also create other sources of livelihood. For this reason, they turned to the chestnut grove. They cleared the wastelands of wild plants, “tamed” the fruit trees by grafting them and transformed the banks of the streams into fertile orchards, where they also cultivated the first potatoes. After the great fire of 1877 (the "yangini" of 14-15/8/1877), the wild chestnuts, which sprouted on the trunks of the old ones, were domesticated with grafts from domestic chestnuts and their production at the end of the Turkish occupation reached three hundred thousand okadas. During the occupation, the chestnut was the product that saved thousands of lives from hunger. And because the Agiasotes, with their intense tymosophic disposition, always satirize both the joys and the sufferings of their lives, they used to say the following quatrain at that time:

_The chestnuts are gone, so be careful_ _not to eat chestnuts, lest this happen to you!_

(Swelling refers to bloating due to hunger, a very common phenomenon in the winter of 1941-1942.)

## The chestnut grove of Agiasos

The surrounding nature of Agiasos constitutes one of the most important protected forest resources of the island and the entire area of Mount Olympus and the Bay of Geras are included in the Natura 2000 network. The vegetation of the area consists of extensive forests of Scots pine (pinus brutia) to the west of the settlement, while on its southern outskirts begins the famous chestnut grove of Agiasos consisting of the common castanea sativa, which is the only one that constitutes a significant forest area on the island. The existence of the chestnut grove of Agiasos there gives the occasion every year, in late October and early November, for the settlement to organize the Chestnut Festival and at the same time walking excursions through the chestnut grove.

This chestnut grove is a rural road that leads through a dense chestnut forest to the Valanos springs, which water part of the village and ends in Ai-Yiannis. It covers 11,000 acres of chestnut forest. The production of chestnuts offers a lot to the residents of Agiasos, since first of all every year the village is flooded with abundant chestnuts and at the same time valuable timber is produced. Its fruit is quite nutritious and thus the tree multiplies quickly and is found at an altitude of 400-1000 meters. It can survive forest fires and is suitable for organic farming.

The production of the product and its quality depend on climatic conditions and early rains. The timber produced from the chestnut tree is also of great value. For years now, this product has been used to decorate the houses of the island with window frames, furniture, roof shingles and sachnisinia, which are the wooden projections of houses.

Although the chestnuts produced in Agiasos are entirely organic - ecological, because their cultivation has been carried out for years in a primitive and opportunistic way, chestnut diseases, the low price of the product, and the lack of compensatory financial benefits lead to a continuous decrease in the total product produced. The main diseases of the Agiasos chestnut grove are fruit rot, ulcer, mistletoe, melanosis, pink and red worm, black aphid, and wood-eating insects.

Nevertheless, the Agiasos chestnut is a sought-after local product, which enjoys the appreciation of the consumer public. Today, many locals and mainly foreign tourists visit Agiasos to get a taste of the idyllic autumn atmosphere created in the large chestnut grove of Lesbos Olympus.

## "Chestnut Festival" in Agiasos

One of the most familiar and beloved images of the transition from Greek autumn to winter, a source of inspiration for painters, writers and songwriters, is that of the chestnut man with the portable brazier, the well-known foufou, roasting chestnuts and offering them hot and dry-roasted or boiled in large steaming cauldrons.

![Chestnuts](/assets/images/cms/giorti-kastanou-4.webp)

The picturesque Agiasos in Lesvos is famous for its beautiful chestnut grove and every November it celebrates the arrival of the new fruits. It began in 2004 and is organized as an institution continuously until today, depending on the harvests of the chestnut producers, usually in the first fortnight of November. It is a huge success and has become the largest autumn institution in Lesvos, with devotees from other parts of Greece and even Turkey.

The "Chestnut Festival" is an agrotourism festival with parallel events, exhibitions and activities around the chestnut, which was co-organized by members of the "Entrepreneurship Animators" network of the Lesvos TEDK in collaboration with the former Municipality of Agiasos and was supported by local associations and some sponsors.

It has been designed according to the standards of other European and Greek agrotourism festivals, intensifying the development of ecological and nature-loving tourism.

## A mental tour of the "Chestnut Festival"

Walking along the dousemedes of Agiasos, streets covered with white cobblestones, in addition to coming into contact with cultural elements of another era such as the "lagadouria", the "kiglitsia" and the "sachnisinia", one can taste the warm and dry-roasted chestnuts from the traditional foufoudes and the boiled cauldrons along with ouzo or wine. One can also be guided along the paths (patoumenis) of the cobblestone chestnut grove and pick chestnuts themselves.

Without forgetting, of course, that Agiasos is the largest center of handicraft tradition on the island with a wide variety of weavers, woodcarvers and ceramics, one can admire the local folklore art by the area's own craftsmen, while at the same time one can watch theatrical and musical performances and taste other exquisite local products in the village taverns with exquisite meze, listen to the famous local dialect of the "Agiasotka" in the village's cobblestone streets and in the Market Square.

And if at the end of the tour you feel exhausted, at the "Theophilos Cafe" or the "Giannakis Cafe" you can drink a coffee on the embers or a kainari to immerse yourself in an exotic, intense flavor of cinnamon, allspice, ginger, mountain tea, sage, cloves and orange peels.
